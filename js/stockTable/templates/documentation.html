Terms defined
This application analyzes the attractiveness of stocks based on the opinions of industry analysts. It takes a stock's 1 year expected price, calculates a discount rate using the stock's beta and the Capital Asset Pricing Model, and determines an individual share's net present value. With this, the application calculates the stock's return on investment, which it compares to other stocks.
Return on Investment (ROI)
A normalized way to compare stocks of different prices to each other, regardless of price. This measure takes the expected return and risk of the company into consideration.
ROI = NPV / Stock price
Internal Rate of Return (IRR)
The effective rate a stock is expected to get. This is the discount rate that a stock would need in order to obtain an NPV of $0. The main difference between IRR and ROI is that ROI takes risk into account, but IRR does not.
IRR = (1 year target price + dividends) / current price
Net Present Value (NPV)
A numerical way to measure the attractiveness of an investment, or in this case, a stock. NPV is a fundamental valuation technique used in finance. NPV measures cash flows, taking the time value of money into account. Here, a simplified, one year model is used to calculate the NPV of each stock.
NPV = (1 year target price + dividends) / (1 + COE)
Time Value of Money
The idea that a dollar today is worth more than a dollar 1 year from now.
Cost of Equity (COE), or Discount Rate
A numerical measure of risk of a stock. The higher the COE, the higher the risk of the stock. A stock that performs similarly to the market will normally have a COE of between 10% and 12%. COE is calculated using the CAPM.
Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM)
Used to calculate the COE of a stock. CAPM uses the risk of the company, as measured by the beta, and the market risk premium to determine a discount rate for the stock.
r_i = r_f + beta_i * (r_m - r_f)

Where:
r_i: discount rate for the stock
r_f: risk free rate
beta_i: beta for the stock
r_m: expected market return
Beta
A measure of the riskiness of the stock, relative to the market.
Beta values:

Greater than 1: Riskier than the market
Equal to 1: As risky as the market
Between 0 and 1: Less risky than the market, but still generally moves with the market
Equal to 0: Uncorrelated with the market
Less than 0: Generally moves against the market. If the market rises, this stock will generally fall
Market Risk Premium
An investor will not want to take on extra risk unless they expect a higher rate of return. The market risk premium is the difference between the market's expected return and the risk free rate of return.
Risk Free Rate
The annualized return on the a riskless asset, usually proxied by the 3 month US Treasury Bill, or the 10 year US Treasury Bond. Here, the 3 month Treasury Bill is used as a proxy for the risk free rate.
Expected Market Return
Proxied by the 1 year historical return on the S&P 500. Ideally, one would want their portfolio, and each individual stock, to outperform the market.
